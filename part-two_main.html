Part II: UP HOT CREEK

Construction had arrived in Mammoth Lakes. Intrawest, a developer notable for shaping a number of North American ski resorts, not only bought a stake in Mammoth Mountain, but also acquired all of the developable real estate owned by the Mountain. They, like the town, wanted to transform Mammoth from a town to a destination.

“Intrawest was firmly entrenched in Mammoth and going forward with its plans,” said Bill Manning, Mammoth Lakes Airport Manager. “A development at any number of levels was on their minds.”

Intrawest’s most prolific project was “The Village at Mammoth,” a downtown promenade similar in design to its other established resorts. The designs included high-end hotels, gourmet restaurants and clothing boutiques. The plans upended Mammoth’s sleepy, sprawling layout in hopes of ushering in a more tourist-friendly and profitable era.

Mammoth chose development to ensure its financial fortunes. But there was a missing link to the grand design: a destination resort cannot function as a destination without an airport with commercial air service.

How would the town attract visitors from around the country without a modern airport?
